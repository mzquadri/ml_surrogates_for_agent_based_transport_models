\subsection{Discussion of Trial Results}

Trial~1 achieves the highest $R^2 = 0.7860$ and lowest MAE $= 2.97$ veh/h among all trials. However, Trial~1 uses a structurally different \texttt{Linear} final layer and zero dropout, making it architecturally incompatible with Trials~2--8 (\texttt{GATConv} final layer, non-zero dropout). This difference has two consequences: first, direct performance comparison between T1 and T2--T8 conflates the effect of the output layer type with other hyperparameter differences; second, MC Dropout UQ is \emph{undefined} for Trial~1 because zero dropout means all stochastic forward passes produce identical outputs, yielding zero variance. Trial~1 is therefore excluded from all UQ experiments as a principled architectural constraint, not as an arbitrary choice.

Among Trials~2--8 (comparable architecture with \texttt{GATConv} output and non-zero dropout), Trial~8 achieves the best test performance: $R^2 = 0.5957$, MAE $= 3.96$ veh/h, RMSE $= 7.12$ veh/h. Trials~3--4 (weighted loss) show markedly lower $R^2$ (0.22--0.24), indicating that weighted MSE loss did not improve generalization on this dataset.

\subsection{Best Model Selection}

Trial~1 achieves the highest absolute scores ($R^2 = 0.7860$, MAE $= 2.97$ veh/h), demonstrating that the underlying feature representations learned by the PointNetTransfGAT backbone are effective. However, Trial~1 is structurally incompatible with MC Dropout uncertainty quantification: its zero effective dropout rate means stochastic forward passes produce no variance, making uncertainty estimation impossible. The exclusion of Trial~1 from UQ experiments is therefore a necessary methodological constraint---MC Dropout requires non-zero dropout by definition~\cite{gal2016dropout}. Among the comparable trials (T2--T8, all using \texttt{GATConv} output with non-zero dropout), Trial~8 is the strongest performer ($R^2 = 0.5957$, MAE $= 3.96$ veh/h) and is therefore used for all UQ experiments. Trial~1's higher accuracy serves as a useful reference point, indicating headroom that future architectural refinements (e.g., combining a linear output head with a separate uncertainty mechanism) might recover.

\section{Ensemble Experiments}

\subsection{Important Caveat: Evaluation Subset Distribution Mismatch}
\label{sec:ensemble_caveat}

The ensemble experiments (Experiment~A and Experiment~B) exhibit near-zero $R^2$ values ($\approx 0.003$ and $-0.002$ respectively). Investigation reveals that this is an artifact of the evaluation subset used for ensemble scoring: the subset of scenarios available at the intersection of all ensemble members' test splits follows a different distribution from the individual models' training data. Crucially, \textbf{this does not indicate that ensembling is ineffective in general}---the same T8 model achieves $R^2 = 0.5957$ in its own standalone evaluation on the correctly matched test split. The near-zero $R^2$ therefore reflects a confound in the evaluation protocol, not a failure of ensemble-based uncertainty estimation.

Spearman~$\rho$ values from ensemble experiments should be interpreted accordingly: they measure ranking quality \emph{within the mismatched subset only}, and likely underestimate the ranking quality that ensemble methods would achieve under a controlled evaluation with matched train/test distributions. Under fair evaluation conditions, ensemble-based uncertainty estimates may well complement or outperform MC Dropout; the present results cannot adjudicate this question.

\subsection{Conditional Coverage by Uncertainty Level}
\label{sec:conformal_conditional}

Standard conformal prediction guarantees \emph{marginal} coverage---averaged over the entire test distribution---but Barber~et~al.~\cite{barber2021limits} prove that distribution-free \emph{conditional} coverage (i.e., coverage conditioned on specific input features or uncertainty levels) is impossible without additional assumptions. This is a fundamental theoretical result, not a limitation specific to our model: \emph{any} distribution-free conformal method applied to heterogeneous data will exhibit conditional coverage variation. In practice, fixed-width conformal intervals inevitably over-cover easy-to-predict subpopulations and under-cover difficult ones.

We evaluate this theoretically expected behaviour by stratifying the evaluation nodes into 10 deciles based on MC Dropout $\sigma$ (D1 = lowest uncertainty, D10 = highest) and computing coverage within each decile using the global conformal quantile.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/t8_conformal_conditional.pdf}
\caption[T8 conditional conformal coverage by uncertainty decile]{Conditional coverage by MC Dropout uncertainty decile for T8. Left: standard global conformal intervals over-cover low-uncertainty nodes (D1: 98.6\% at 90\% nominal) and under-cover high-uncertainty nodes (D10: 62.9\%). Right: adaptive conformal prediction, which normalises residuals by $\sigma$ before computing the quantile, substantially reduces this disparity. Based on 1,000 of 10,000 available MATSim scenarios (10\% subset).}
\label{fig:conformal_conditional}
\end{figure}

Standard conformal intervals exhibit the conditional coverage variation predicted by theory: at the 90\% nominal level, coverage ranges from 98.6\% (D1) to 62.9\% (D10). This is the expected consequence of applying fixed-width intervals to a heterogeneous prediction task---the interval is unnecessarily wide for well-predicted nodes and too narrow for difficult ones. \textbf{Adaptive conformal prediction}, which uses $|y - \hat{y}|/\sigma$ as the nonconformity score instead of $|y - \hat{y}|$, provides a principled solution: by normalising residuals by the MC Dropout uncertainty, it produces node-specific interval widths that are wider where the model is less certain and narrower where it is confident. This reduces the conditional coverage disparity to a narrow range: coverage ranges from 90.0\% to 96.2\% across deciles at the 90\% level, while preserving the marginal coverage guarantee. The adaptive conformal quantile ($q_{90}^\text{adapt} = 7.58$) is close to the empirical $k_{90}$ from the calibration audit, confirming internal consistency between the MC Dropout ranking and the conformal calibration.

\subsection{MC Dropout as a Ranking Signal vs Calibrated Distribution}

Although MC Dropout uncertainty $\sigma$ correlates meaningfully with errors ($\rho = 0.4820$), the raw $\sigma$ values are \textbf{not} calibrated standard deviations---a well-documented property of approximate Bayesian inference methods~\cite{gal2016dropout, laves2020well}. Table~\ref{tab:k95} shows the scaling factor required to achieve 95\% coverage using $\pm k \sigma$ intervals.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{figures/fig7_calibration.pdf}
\caption[Scaling factor $k_{95}$ for 95\% coverage: Gaussian vs T8]{Scaling factor $k_{95}$ required for $\pm k\sigma$ intervals to achieve 95\% coverage. The $k_{95} = 11.34$ result quantifies a known theoretical limitation of MC Dropout: the variational posterior that dropout approximates is typically too concentrated, producing uncertainty estimates that are informative in \emph{rank order} but underestimated in \emph{absolute magnitude}~\cite{gal2016dropout}. This does not diminish the practical value of MC Dropout for selective prediction ($-$41.6\% MAE at 50\% retention) or error detection (AUROC = 0.76); it means that the raw $\sigma$ is most effective as a \emph{ranking signal} for identifying which predictions are more or less reliable, rather than as a calibrated probability distribution. Sections~\ref{sec:coverage_calibration}--\ref{sec:temperature_scaling} and the conformal prediction results in Section~\ref{sec:conformal_results} demonstrate how this limitation is addressed through post-hoc calibration and distribution-free coverage guarantees.

The pattern is consistent across all levels: MC Dropout intervals fall well short of their nominal coverage. At the 50\% level, $\pm 0.674\sigma$ achieves only 23.3\% coverage---less than half the target. The gap does not close at higher nominal levels; the 95\% interval achieves only 54.8\% coverage. This systematic under-coverage reflects the fact that MC Dropout $\sigma$ values are too small in absolute terms, with a mean of $\bar{\sigma} = 1.369$~veh/h across the 3,163,500-node evaluation. The cost is much wider fixed intervals: 29.35~veh/h at 95\% vs 5.36~veh/h for the uncalibrated MC Dropout interval. These two methods are therefore complementary rather than competing: MC Dropout provides adaptive, node-specific uncertainty scores useful for ranking and selective filtering, while conformal prediction provides marginal coverage guarantees at the cost of interval adaptivity.

\section{Reliability Diagram and Calibration Curve}
\label{sec:reliability_diagram}

The coverage calibration analysis in the previous section examines calibration at a few discrete nominal levels. A \emph{reliability diagram}~\cite{kuleshov2018accurate, naeini2015obtaining} provides a more comprehensive view by sweeping across all nominal coverage levels from 10\% to 95\%. For each nominal level~$p$, we compute the Gaussian quantile $z_p = \Phi^{-1}((1+p)/2)$ and measure the fraction of nodes where $|y - \hat{y}_\text{MC}| \leq z_p \cdot \sigma_\text{MC}$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/t8_reliability_diagram.pdf}
\caption[T8 reliability diagram: expected vs observed Gaussian coverage]{Left: reliability diagram for T8 MC Dropout ($S=30$, 100 graphs, 3,163,500 nodes). The diagonal represents perfect calibration; the model consistently undercovers. Right: coverage gap in percentage points at each nominal level. ECE = 0.265. Based on 1,000 of 10,000 available MATSim scenarios (10\% subset).}
\label{fig:reliability_diagram}
\end{figure}

The Expected Calibration Error (ECE), defined as the mean absolute difference between nominal and observed coverage across all levels, is $\text{ECE} = 0.265$ (computed over all 100 test graphs; the 20-graph calibration subset used for temperature scaling in Section~\ref{sec:temperature_scaling} yields a comparable ECE of 0.269). This level of miscalibration is consistent with the $k_{95} = 11.34$ finding and is characteristic of MC Dropout as an approximate Bayesian method: the variational posterior underestimates uncertainty in absolute terms at every nominal level. Importantly, this miscalibration does not invalidate the uncertainty \emph{ranking}---Spearman~$\rho = 0.4820$ and the selective prediction results (Section~\ref{sec:selective_prediction}) demonstrate that the ranking signal remains highly useful despite the absolute miscalibration. The following section addresses this calibration gap through post-hoc temperature scaling.

\subsection{Post-Hoc Temperature Scaling}
\label{sec:temperature_scaling}

Temperature scaling~\cite{guo2017calibration, laves2020well} is a post-hoc recalibration method that scales all uncertainty estimates by a single learned parameter $T$: $\sigma_\text{scaled} = \sigma_\text{raw} \cdot T$. We optimise $T$ on a calibration set (first 20 test graphs, 632,700 nodes) by minimising the Kuleshov ECE, and evaluate on the remaining 80 test graphs (2,530,800 nodes).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/t8_temperature_scaling.pdf}
\caption[T8 temperature scaling: reliability diagram before and after calibration]{Temperature scaling calibration for T8. Top: reliability diagrams before (left, ECE = 0.269) and after (right, ECE = 0.048) scaling with $T = 2.70$. Bottom left: overlay comparison. Bottom right: coverage gap reduction at each nominal level. Evaluation on 80 held-out graphs (2,530,800 nodes). Based on 1,000 of 10,000 available MATSim scenarios (10\% subset).}
\label{fig:temperature_scaling}
\end{figure}

The optimal temperature is $T = 2.70$, indicating that raw MC Dropout standard deviations need to be scaled by a factor of approximately 2.7 to approach calibration. This reduces the ECE from 0.269 to 0.048---an \textbf{82\% improvement}---on the held-out evaluation set. However, residual miscalibration persists, particularly at high nominal levels: the scaled 95\% interval achieves only 83.3\% coverage instead of 95\%. The effective $k_{95}$ after scaling is $z_{0.975} \times T = 1.96 \times 2.70 = 5.30$, which is closer to but still below the empirical $k_{95} = 11.34$. This residual gap reflects the non-Gaussian shape of the residual distribution: temperature scaling assumes the errors are proportional to a Gaussian scaled by $\sigma$, but the empirical distribution has heavier tails than any single-parameter Gaussian rescaling can capture.

Temperature scaling therefore provides a meaningful but incomplete improvement. For applications requiring trustworthy coverage, conformal prediction remains necessary.
